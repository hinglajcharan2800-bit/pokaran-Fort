History of the Fort

Pokhran Fort, also known as Balagarh, is a 14th-century fort built by the Rathore clan in Rajasthan, known for its intricate Rajput architecture, dual residential and defensive roles, and a museum showcasing royal artifacts. It is located in the town of Pokhran, famous for the nearby Pokhran Test Range, where India conducted its first nuclear test, codenamed "Smiling Buddha," in 1974. Notable features Museum: The fort complex includes a museum that displays a collection of royal armor, weapons, and Pokhran pottery. Murals: The fort walls are adorned with beautifully crafted murals that depict various aspects of Rajput culture and history. Temple: A small red sandstone temple dedicated to Goddess Durga is also part of the fort complex. Connection to nuclear tests Location: The nearby Pokhran Test Range is famous as the site of India's first nuclear test on May 18, 1974, codenamed "Smiling Buddha". Significance: This test marked India's entry into the group of nuclear-powered nations.

History and architecture

Construction: The fort was built in the 14th century by the Rathore clan of Rajputs, though some sources attribute its construction to Rao Maldeo. Purpose: It served a dual purpose as a residential palace for the ruling family and a defensive stronghold. Architectural style: It showcases detailed Rajput architecture with grand gates, intricate carvings, and jharokhas (overhanging enclosed balconies). Location: The fort is situated in Pokhran, a town in Rajasthan, which is also the site of the Pokhran Test Range.
